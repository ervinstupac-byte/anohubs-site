2. Holistic Problem Breakdown: From Root Cause to Consequence

The biggest problem is not that parts wear out, but that the **root causes** of that wear are often ignored. These issues manifest in a critical chain of cause and effect:

A. Initial Problems (Root Causes)

  1. Assembly and Installation Errors: Errors such as poor shaft alignment (the source of the 48% dynamic risk) or improper bolt tightening become a "ticking time bomb."
  2. Electrical Factors: Problems with grounding, faulty protection, or inadequate automation lead to excessive mechanical stress, vibrations, and **electro-erosion on bearings**.
  3. Inadequate Materials: The use of cheaper, low-quality materials and parts that do not meet specifications due to cost reduction pressure.
  4. Lack of Training: Inexperienced personnel are the direct cause of assembly errors and maintenance oversights.

B. Consequences of Initial Problems (The Silent Killers)

  • Misalignment: Causes uneven load distribution and excessive vibrations, drastically reducing the lifespan of bearings and seals.
  • Vibrations: The "silent killer." They cause material fatigue and destroy critical components.
  • Contamination: Water, dirt, or foreign particles entering lubrication systems destroy oil properties, leading to rapid wear of critical components.

C. Phase of Critical Failure (Terminal Phase)

Problems escalate, leading to catastrophic results:

  • Cavitation and Erosion: Poor alignment of turbine blades leads to the formation of vapor bubbles (cavitation), destroying the rotor and reducing efficiency.
  • Bearing Failure: Bearings, stressed by vibrations and contaminated oil, fail, leading to overheating and the complete destruction of the shaft or generator.

3. The Human and Strategic Factor: The True Root Cause

Technical failures are often a symptom of deeper organizational and human issues. This is where the true cost of compromise is found:

The Critical Human Factors:

  • **Reactive vs. Proactive Culture:** A culture prioritizing short-term savings guarantees addressing problems only after they become expensive failures.
  • **The Cost of Inexperienced Personnel:** Savings from cheaper labor are instantly overshadowed by the costs of extensive repairs caused by mistakes.
  • **The "Burnout" Factor:** Pressure on key experts leads to high stress and loss of know-how due to retirement or resignation.
  • **Communication Gap:** Lack of real-time communication between site teams and engineering delays repairs and increases risk.
